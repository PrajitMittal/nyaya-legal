# Indian Legal System: Raw Pain Points Research
## Extensive Web Research - April 2026

---

## SECTION 1: PROBLEMS FACED BY LAWYERS

### 1.1 Adjournment Culture (The #1 Daily Frustration)
- **Problem**: 61% of ALL hearings result in adjournments. Average of 32 adjournments granted per case. Advocates responsible for 58% of adjournment requests.
- **Who it affects**: Lawyers, litigants, judges
- **Source**: DAKSH India study (dakshindia.org/Justice-Frustrated)
- **Severity**: EXTREME - 33% of judicial time in criminal courts is spent purely on adjournments. A case that should take 1 year stretches to 5+ years.
- **Breakdown of causes**: 44% absent party/advocate, 20% absent witnesses, 16% judge absent, 14% parties seeking extra time
- **Existing tools**: None that effectively address this

### 1.2 No Digital Case Management Tools
- **Problem**: Legal professionals still rely on manual methods for research, documentation, and case management. No intelligent scheduling systems. Physical files dominate. Courts lack automated document review and storage.
- **Who it affects**: Lawyers (especially solo practitioners and small firms)
- **Source**: LiveLaw (livelaw.in), Law.Asia Indian Legal Market 2024 report
- **Severity**: HIGH - Time-consuming, error-prone, makes scaling impossible
- **Existing tools**: Some paid tools (Manupatra, LegitQuest) exist for legal research but NOT for daily case management, hearing tracking, or client communication

### 1.3 Running Between Multiple Courts
- **Problem**: Lawyers handle cases across different courts (district, sessions, high court, tribunals) on the same day. Cause lists are published late. Over 100 matters may be listed before a single bench. Cases sometimes heard on different dates than scheduled without notice.
- **Who it affects**: Litigation lawyers
- **Source**: Legally India forums, DAKSH study
- **Severity**: HIGH - Lawyers waste hours waiting. Only 6 of 50 listed cases typically get hearings. Average judge handles 64 cases/day with ~3.5 minutes per case.
- **Existing tools**: eCourts app shows cause lists but does NOT send smart notifications, does NOT help with scheduling conflicts

### 1.4 First-Generation Lawyers Face Access Deficits
- **Problem**: No family connections = slower client acquisition, no referral networks, steeper learning curve on unwritten court norms, resource constraints for office space and law libraries
- **Who it affects**: New lawyers without legal family background
- **Source**: Legally India, Quora discussions
- **Severity**: HIGH - Survival rate is very low. Starting salary as low as Rs 5,000-15,000/month in litigation
- **Existing tools**: None specifically addressing this gap

### 1.5 Low Pay and Financial Struggles
- **Problem**: Litigation lawyers at junior levels earn Rs 50,000-60,000/month at firms, far less in independent practice. Legal aid lawyers earn Rs 4,000-5,000/month. No benefits, no maternity leave, no sick leave, no social security.
- **Who it affects**: Junior lawyers, legal aid panel lawyers
- **Source**: Legally India salary threads, IDR Online (idronline.org)
- **Severity**: HIGH - Forces talented lawyers out of litigation and legal aid entirely
- **Existing tools**: None

### 1.6 Language Barriers
- **Problem**: Supreme Court and High Courts operate in English, which CJI described as incomprehensible to "99.9% of citizens." Lawyers from non-English backgrounds struggle. Study materials in regional languages are scarce. Translation of legal nuance is frequently lost.
- **Who it affects**: Lawyers from rural/vernacular backgrounds, litigants
- **Source**: Supreme Court Observer, LocalSamosa
- **Severity**: HIGH - Creates two-tier legal profession; rural litigants cannot understand their own cases
- **Existing tools**: SC translating judgments into 14 languages (21,000+ done), but proceedings still in English

### 1.7 Court Infrastructure Failures
- **Problem**: Lifts restricted to judges only. No litigant waiting rooms. Missing wheelchair accessibility. Poor signage. Missing stenographers and typists delay order typing. 25%+ staff vacancy in high courts. 14.7% courtroom shortage nationally.
- **Who it affects**: Lawyers, litigants, disabled persons
- **Source**: DAKSH Faridabad study, BharatNama Substack
- **Severity**: MEDIUM-HIGH - Daily physical discomfort and operational delays
- **Existing tools**: None

## SECTION 2: PROBLEMS FACED BY POLICE OFFICERS

### 2.1 CCTNS Software is Crash-Prone and Unstable
- **Problem**: The Crime and Criminal Tracking Network System (CCTNS), meant to connect 14,000+ police stations, has unstable Core Application Software (CAS). Data migration errors. Integration with legacy systems creates bottlenecks. Inconsistent data standardization across states.
- **Who it affects**: Police officers, investigating officers
- **Source**: Wikipedia CCTNS, MHA documents, Testbook
- **Severity**: EXTREME - Originally due in 2012, still not fully functional. Crash-prone software forces officers back to manual registers.
- **Existing tools**: CCTNS itself IS the tool, but it barely works

### 2.2 FIR Registration Corruption and Refusal
- **Problem**: Non-registration of FIR is one of the biggest sources of police corruption. Officers refuse FIRs for cognizable offenses. Period payments are demanded to keep investigations moving. A slip in bribe schedule = case dropped from priority.
- **Who it affects**: Police officers (honest ones forced into corrupt system), citizens
- **Source**: SVPNPA study on police corruption, iPleaders, Haryana ACB data
- **Severity**: EXTREME - 34 of 104 bribery trap cases in Haryana 2024 were against police. Systemic corruption.
- **Existing tools**: Zero FIR provision exists in law but poorly implemented. e-FIR portals exist in some states but not universal.

### 2.3 Chargesheet Filing Delays
- **Problem**: Police must file chargesheets within 60-90 days but routinely fail. In Assam, 59% of cases remain uninvestigated. Low police-population ratio (182 per 100,000 vs UN norm of 222). 60% of all arrests are deemed unnecessary.
- **Who it affects**: Police, accused persons, judiciary
- **Source**: ORF India, Supreme Court judgments, LawAndOtherThings
- **Severity**: EXTREME - Accused get default bail when chargesheet is late (NEET case 2024). Supplementary chargesheets abused to prolong detention.
- **Existing tools**: CCTNS supposed to track this but doesn't effectively

### 2.4 Case Diary Maintenance is Manual and Opaque
- **Problem**: Every IO must maintain daily case diary with timestamps, places visited, circumstances. This is entirely manual. No digital system. Diaries are not accessible to accused. Courts can demand them but process is cumbersome. Web scraping of digital evidence gets lost due to inexperienced handling.
- **Who it affects**: Investigating officers, defense lawyers, accused
- **Source**: iPleaders, LawCtopus, LiveLaw
- **Severity**: HIGH - Critical evidence lost. No accountability. No real-time tracking of investigation progress.
- **Existing tools**: None - entirely manual process

### 2.5 Evidence Management and Digital Forensics Gap
- **Problem**: Data lost on hard drives due to electromagnetic forces in police storage. No specialized digital forensics training. Chain of custody for digital evidence is poorly maintained. Physical evidence storage is inadequate.
- **Who it affects**: Police, prosecutors, accused
- **Source**: NUJS research paper, IndiaAI.gov.in
- **Severity**: HIGH - Cases collapse due to evidence mishandling. Digital evidence increasingly critical but police unequipped.
- **Existing tools**: Very limited - no standardized digital evidence management system

### 2.6 Funding and Resource Crisis
- **Problem**: Maharashtra spends 98% of police budget on salaries/fuel, only 2% on investigator training. Most states allocate only 3-5% of budget to policing. Public prosecutors lack legal databases, research assistants, or office space.
- **Who it affects**: Police officers, prosecutors
- **Source**: ORF India research
- **Severity**: HIGH - Structurally impossible to do quality investigations
- **Existing tools**: None

## SECTION 3: PROBLEMS FACED BY CITIZENS/LITIGANTS

### 3.1 Extreme Case Delays
- **Problem**: 50+ million cases pending. 180,000+ cases pending for 30+ years. UP High Court cases average 11 years. Property/civil disputes take 10-20 years. Contract enforcement takes ~4 years vs 164 days in Singapore. Calcutta HC has 94% of cases pending 50+ years.
- **Who it affects**: All litigants
- **Source**: NJDG data, BharatNama Substack, StudyIQ, Wikipedia
- **Severity**: CATASTROPHIC - Judicial delays cost India 2%+ of GDP. Litigants die before getting justice. Businesses write off disputes rather than go to court.
- **Existing tools**: None that meaningfully address this

### 3.2 Undertrial Prisoner Crisis
- **Problem**: 75.8% of India's prison population are undertrials (unconvicted). Birbal Bhagat spent 26 YEARS in jail before acquittal. 24,879 accused granted bail but remain in custody because they can't furnish bail bonds. Bail approval: only 12% at first hearing. Muslims, Dalits, Adivasis disproportionately affected.
- **Who it affects**: Accused persons (mostly poor), their families
- **Source**: ORF India, InsightsOnIndia, Bar and Bench, The Wire
- **Severity**: CATASTROPHIC - Constitutional rights violation on massive scale. People serve more time as undertrials than their potential sentence.
- **Existing tools**: Section 436A CrPC exists but implementation is negligible. LADCS scheme launched 2022 but outcome monitoring not established.

### 3.3 Cannot Afford Bail Even When Granted
- **Problem**: 24,879 accused who were granted bail continue to be in custody because they cannot furnish bail bonds. Allahabad alone has 6,158 such cases. System requires sureties that poor people cannot provide.
- **Who it affects**: Poor accused persons
- **Source**: Supreme Court data via Bar and Bench
- **Severity**: EXTREME - People rot in jail despite being legally entitled to bail
- **Existing tools**: Personal bond provisions exist but judges rarely apply them

### 3.4 Legal Aid is Practically Useless
- **Problem**: Only 7.91% of undertrials access free legal aid. Budget: Rs 4.57 per person per year. Panel lawyers earn Rs 5,000/month. Only 20% of legal aid lawyers get formal training. Lawyers meet undertrials infrequently, are unaware of case histories. 75% of rural citizens unaware of free legal aid entitlement. Only 1 legal aid clinic per 163 villages.
- **Who it affects**: Poor litigants, undertrials, marginalized communities
- **Source**: IDR Online, Bar and Bench, NALSA data, InsightsOnIndia
- **Severity**: EXTREME - Constitutional right to legal aid exists on paper but is dysfunctional in practice. Qualified lawyers avoid legal aid work entirely.
- **Existing tools**: NALSA exists but severely underfunded and understaffed

### 3.5 Police Station Harassment and Bribery
- **Problem**: 42% of people surveyed paid bribes to police. Investigation proceeds only if "subordinate police functionaries are greased." Period payments required to keep paperwork moving. 63% of people fear retaliation for reporting corruption.
- **Who it affects**: Citizens filing complaints, victims of crime
- **Source**: Transparency International via Scroll.in, SVPNPA research, ACB Haryana data
- **Severity**: EXTREME - First point of contact with justice system is deeply corrupt. Victims become secondary victims.
- **Existing tools**: ACB complaints possible but feared. WhatsApp-based reporting launched in some states.

### 3.6 Cannot Understand Own Case
- **Problem**: Proceedings in English/legal jargon. Judgments not in litigant's language. 7 of 15 litigants surveyed felt lawyers failed to brief them on case progress. Citizens cannot independently understand their rights or legal steps without expensive lawyer consultation.
- **Who it affects**: All litigants, especially rural and non-English speakers
- **Source**: DAKSH Faridabad study, Supreme Court Observer, LiveLaw
- **Severity**: HIGH - People go through years of litigation without understanding what's happening in their case
- **Existing tools**: SC translating some judgments. No tool simplifies real-time case proceedings for litigants.

### 3.7 Court Summons/Notice Service Failure
- **Problem**: No guidelines for process servers. Difficult to identify persons to be served. Cases where litigants never received summons but arrest warrants issued. If reason cited is "refusal," summons deemed delivered even if person was never found. Process servers corrupt.
- **Who it affects**: Defendants, litigants
- **Source**: West Bengal Judicial Academy, Lexology, LiveLaw
- **Severity**: HIGH - People get ex parte orders passed against them without ever knowing about the case
- **Existing tools**: NSTEP app for GPS tracking of process servers launched but adoption unclear

### 3.8 Finding a Trustworthy Lawyer is a Nightmare
- **Problem**: No standardized rating system. No mandatory fee transparency. No way to verify track record. Lawyers make false promises. No accountability for delays caused by own lawyer. Fee structures opaque - range from Rs 1,000 to Rs 30,000/hour with no benchmarks.
- **Who it affects**: All litigants
- **Source**: LegalReader, Karl Rock blog, Kaanoon.com
- **Severity**: HIGH - Wrong lawyer choice = years of wasted time and money
- **Existing tools**: LawRato, MyAdvo etc. exist but have minimal verified reviews and no outcome data

### 3.9 Property Disputes are Endless
- **Problem**: Land/property disputes take average 20 YEARS to resolve. Only 50% of mutation records computerized. Land records unreliable. 26% of pending cases (8.5 million) are over 5 years old. No conclusive land titling system.
- **Who it affects**: Property owners, buyers, inheritors
- **Source**: PRS India, Landeed.com, CMA Knowledge
- **Severity**: EXTREME - Most common type of civil dispute, affects millions of families. Entire generations live in uncertainty.
- **Existing tools**: DILRMP (Digital India Land Records Modernization) underway but far from complete

### 3.10 Cybercrime Complaints Go Nowhere
- **Problem**: 19.18 lakh cybercrime complaints in 2024, losses of Rs 22,811.95 crore. Simple cases take 7-15 days for initial action. Complex cases take 6-18 months. Police lack expertise. FIR delays are common.
- **Who it affects**: Cybercrime victims
- **Source**: MedaName, DDNews, CyberCrime.gov.in
- **Severity**: HIGH - 500% increase in cybercrime 2021-2024 but response infrastructure hasn't kept pace
- **Existing tools**: National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal exists but police follow-up is the bottleneck

## SECTION 4: SYSTEMIC/TECHNOLOGY PROBLEMS

### 4.1 eCourts Data Quality is Questionable
- **Problem**: No data quality audits performed. Statute names and section numbers left blank (non-mandatory fields). Lack of system integration creates data mismatches. No standard data quality manuals exist.
- **Who it affects**: Everyone who relies on eCourts data - lawyers, litigants, researchers
- **Source**: NIPFP Working Paper 314 (2020)
- **Severity**: HIGH - Decisions made on unreliable data. Case tracking can be wrong.
- **Existing tools**: eCourts portal itself has the problems

### 4.2 Indian Kanoon Limitations
- **Problem**: Cannot be officially cited as a source. Web scraping errors not manually corrected. No notion of relevance in search (just keyword matching). Misspelling of Indian names creates search failures. Not accepted by judges as official source.
- **Who it affects**: Lawyers, law students, self-represented litigants
- **Source**: NBER research paper, Law Cornell VoxPopuLII
- **Severity**: MEDIUM - It's free and useful but not reliable enough for court use. Paid alternatives (SCC Online, Manupatra) are expensive.
- **Existing tools**: Indian Kanoon IS the free tool; paid alternatives exist but cost Rs 10,000-50,000+/year

### 4.3 NCRB Data is Always 2 Years Outdated
- **Problem**: Crime statistics take average 22 months to publish. 2023 report still not released as of early 2025. Data is secondary (compiled from police submissions). NCRB disclaims responsibility for data authenticity. Stopped publishing percentage-per-state data from 2020.
- **Who it affects**: Policymakers, researchers, media, citizens
- **Source**: Factly.in, CJP, ORF India
- **Severity**: MEDIUM-HIGH - Policy decisions made on 2+ year old data. Trends invisible when needed most.
- **Existing tools**: NCRB is the only source. No real-time crime data alternative.

### 4.4 No Integration Between Police-Court-Prison Systems
- **Problem**: Prisons track lawyer presence but not quality of representation. Courts track proceedings but not counsel-client interactions. District Legal Services Authorities track appointments separately. "These lines rarely converge." CCTNS, eCourts, e-Prisons are siloed systems.
- **Who it affects**: Everyone in the criminal justice chain
- **Source**: Bar and Bench, ORF India
- **Severity**: EXTREME - No single view of a case across investigation -> prosecution -> trial -> prison. Undertrial eligibility for release falls through cracks.
- **Existing tools**: ICJS (Inter-operable Criminal Justice System) launched but integration is poor

### 4.5 Lok Adalats Prioritize Quantity Over Quality
- **Problem**: Enormous pressure to show case disposal numbers. Cases processed in assembly-line fashion without individual consideration. Settlement pressure on vulnerable parties. Systemic problems when dispute resolution becomes volume-driven.
- **Who it affects**: Litigants (especially weaker parties in disputes)
- **Source**: Jus Corpus, Fordham International Law Journal
- **Severity**: MEDIUM-HIGH - Justice traded for statistics
- **Existing tools**: None addressing quality oversight

### 4.6 Government is Party in ~50% of Cases
- **Problem**: Government routinely appeals trivial verdicts. One department sues another. Former CJI NV Ramana said "if the government stopped fighting unnecessary cases, half the judiciary's load would disappear."
- **Who it affects**: All litigants (delays caused by government cases affect everyone)
- **Source**: BharatNama Substack, multiple news sources
- **Severity**: EXTREME - Single biggest contributor to backlog
- **Existing tools**: Government litigation policy exists on paper but poorly enforced

## SECTION 6: GAPS WHERE NO EXISTING TOOL EXISTS

### For Lawyers:
1. **Smart hearing tracker** - No tool sends proactive alerts about cause list changes, adjournments, or scheduling conflicts across courts
2. **Case management for Indian litigation** - No affordable tool designed for Indian court workflow (adjournment tracking, next date tracking, document management per hearing)
3. **Client communication tool** - No standardized way to update clients about case progress in their language
4. **Legal research in vernacular** - No tool provides case law search in Hindi/regional languages with quality results

### For Police:
5. **Working case management software** - CCTNS is broken. No reliable digital FIR-to-chargesheet tracking
6. **Digital evidence management** - No chain-of-custody tracking for digital evidence
7. **Investigation timeline tracker** - No tool alerts IOs about chargesheet deadlines approaching
8. **Witness management** - No system to track and summon witnesses effectively

### For Citizens:
9. **Lawyer marketplace with verified outcomes** - No platform shows lawyer success rates or verified reviews
10. **Case status explained in plain language** - eCourts shows raw data, no one explains "what this means for you"
11. **Bail eligibility calculator** - No tool tells undertrials/families if they qualify for bail under various provisions
12. **FIR filing assistant** - No tool helps citizens understand if their complaint is cognizable, draft the FIR, or know their rights if police refuse
13. **Legal aid connector** - No effective way to connect eligible persons with legal aid services
14. **Property dispute document checker** - No tool helps verify land records, mutation status, and identify potential disputes before purchase
